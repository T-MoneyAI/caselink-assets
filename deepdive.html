1. Business Model Assessment

Is $399-$499/month right for specialists?

Short answer: It's probably $100-$200 too low, but not so wrong that it's killing you.

Let's ground this in what the product is worth. A specialist who adds just 3 net new referred patients per month from better referral relationships generates:
- Orthodontics: 3 × $6,000 = $18,000 additional revenue
- Oral surgery: 3 × $2,000 = $6,000
- Endodontics: 3 × $1,200 = $3,600

Even at the lowest case, $399/month is a 10:1 ROI. That means the price isn't being limited by value — it's being limited by willingness to pay for an unproven tool. This actually suggests a price escalation strategy: start at $399, prove ROI with data, raise to $599-$799 after 6 months, with annual lock-in.

Comparable pricing benchmarks:
- Dental Intelligence: ~$400-$800/month (analytics/production tracking)
- Weave (communications): $400-$600/month
- NexHealth (patient scheduling/communication): $500-$1,000/month
- Archy (full PMS): ~$600-$900/month
- RevenueWell (marketing): $300-$600/month
- ReferralMD (broader healthcare): $500-$2,000/month depending on features

You're priced like a communications tool, but you should be priced like a growth tool. That reframe matters.

Annual pricing: Your website offers 20% off annually (saving $1,197 — which implies you're calculating on $499 not $399; another inconsistency). Annual deals should be your default push. It improves cash flow and reduces churn. Offer 2 months free instead of 20% — it's the same math but feels bigger.

Is free for GPs the right call?

Yes. Unequivocally. Here's why:
- GPs are the supply side of your marketplace. Making them pay creates friction that kills network density.
- Every GP who signs up for free is a locked-in referral channel for paying specialists.
- Free GPs are a moat. Once a GP has their referral workflow in CaseLink, they'll push specialists to join because it makes their life easier.

When should GPs pay?
- Tier 1 (free): Unlimited referrals, basic tracking, HIPAA compliance
- Tier 2 ($49-99/month): Referral analytics ("which specialists accept fastest? best outcomes?"), multi-provider access within a practice, priority routing
- Tier 3 ($199/month): Full two-way communication suite, insurance pre-auth status, patient communication layer

Don't launch GP pricing yet. Get to 100 paying specialists first. Then monetize GPs who have real volume — they'll see the value clearly by then.

LTV of a specialist customer

Assumptions:
- Monthly churn rate: 2% (industry average for B2B SaaS is 2-3%)
- Average price: $449/month (blended, accounting for some annual plans)
- Gross margin: ~80% (software, minimal COGS)

LTV calculation:
- Monthly gross profit: $449 × 80% = $359
- Average customer lifetime: 1/0.02 = 50 months
- LTV = $359 × 50 = ~$17,950 per specialist

If you improve churn to 1% (which is achievable once you add sticky integrations):
- Lifetime = 100 months
- LTV = ~$35,900 per specialist

Target blended LTV: $20,000-$25,000. That's the number to use with investors.

Revenue model additions worth exploring

  1. Transaction fee on referrals — Don't do this. Doctors hate it and it creates the wrong incentive. It also triggers Stark Law / anti-kickback scrutiny.
  2. Scheduling/booking fee — $2-5 per appointment booked through the platform. Low enough to not create friction, adds up at scale.
  3. Insurance pre-auth service — Huge unmet pain. Charge a per-authorization fee ($15-25) or a monthly bundle. This alone could become a second revenue stream that eclipses the subscription.
  4. Data products — Anonymized referral benchmarks sold to DSOs, insurance companies, practice consultants. Long-term play, not 2026 priority.
  5. DSO enterprise tier — Custom pricing at $5,000-$15,000/month per group covering unlimited locations. This is the fastest path to big ARR.

2. Competitive Landscape

Direct Competitors

ReferralMD — The closest to a real competitor, but they're playing a different game. They're targeting health systems and enterprise networks — hospital-affiliated dental practices, FQHCs, large integrated networks. Their pricing starts at ~$500/month and goes to enterprise pricing quickly. Weakness: they're not dental-specific. Their workflow is built for medical specialty referrals (orthopedics, neurology, etc.) and the dental UX feels bolted on. CaseLink wins on dental-specific features and simplicity.

Archy, NexHealth, Weave, Lighthouse 360 — These are full or partial dental PMS/communication platforms that could add referral management. None have done it well yet. The risk: once a practice runs Archy for scheduling + communication + imaging, adding a "send referral" button is not a big lift. This is your most serious long-term threat, not a current one.

Old-school workflow: fax, phone, paper slips — This is your real competitor today. Roughly 60-70% of dental referrals still happen by phone call, paper slip, or fax. This is both the opportunity and the challenge. Behavioral change is hard.

Indirect Competitors

  • Dentrix, Eaglesoft (Patterson), Curve Dental, Open Dental — PMS incumbents. They own the scheduling/billing workflow and could launch referral tracking. Open Dental is open-source and already has a referral tracking module (basic). Dentrix's referral feature is clunky but exists.
  • Epic (for hospital-based dental) — Not your market today. Irrelevant for private practice.
  • WhatsApp/Signal group chats — You'd be surprised. Many dental offices informally coordinate referrals via text. This is a real "invisible" competitor.

Where CaseLink is defensible

  • First-mover in dental-specific referral coordination
  • Network effect (more below)
  • HIPAA compliance as table stakes that most informal solutions lack
  • Analytics layer (which specialist has best outcomes, fastest appointments, capacity data) — this becomes irreplaceable once populated

Where CaseLink is exposed

  • Any PMS adding a basic referral feature undercuts the "we only do referrals" pitch
  • Price sensitivity in solo/small practices
  • DSOs building internal tools (they have the engineering budget and the incentive)

What it takes for an incumbent to replicate this

Dentrix or Eaglesoft could build this in 6-12 months technically. But they won't, for three reasons:
1. They're not incentivized — referral coordination doesn't directly increase their per-seat revenue
2. They'd have to build the two-sided network (GPs AND specialists) — much harder than building software
3. They move slowly and would build something ugly

The window is 18-36 months before a well-funded PMS competitor could take this seriously. Use the time wisely.

3. Network Effect Analysis

How strong is the network effect?

The honest answer: It's local, not national, and it's weaker than you'd like — right now.

Here's the structure: A specialist in Bethesda, MD doesn't benefit from CaseLink adding specialists in Phoenix. The value is entirely local. A GP in Arlington, VA cares whether the oral surgeons and orthodontists they actually refer to are on the platform.

This means your network effect is geographic cluster-based. You need to achieve density within a metro before it compounds. The DMV pilot is the right call, but you need to think about what density looks like.

Density math for DMV:
- DMV has roughly 3,000-4,000 GPs and 800-1,200 specialists
- To be defensible in DMV, you probably need ~40% of specialists in each specialty category (top 3 choices covered)
- In orthodontics: ~100 orthos in DMV. You need 40+ to be "the" platform
- At 100 paying specialists, if most are in DMV, you're approaching defensibility

What breaks the network effect:
1. A specialist leaving and taking their GP relationships with them
2. A competing platform offering "bring your whole network for free" and subsidizing specialists
3. A PMS incumbent adding referral features and bundling them
4. GP practice consolidation into DSOs that use proprietary referral routing

How to make the network effect stronger:
- Make specialist profiles public and searchable (creates organic SEO + lock-in for specialists who have reviews/ratings)
- Build a GP leaderboard ("Dr. Smith sent 47 referrals this month — your top referral source")
- Add two-way outcome tracking ("Your 12 ortho referrals this quarter had 94% case acceptance")
- Create "Preferred Network" features where specialists can offer GPs priority scheduling — this makes a GP's network deeply personal and not portable

4. Blind Spots

#1: The Regulatory Minefield You're Not Taking Seriously Enough

Stark Law / Anti-Kickback adjacent risk: Dental is not subject to federal Stark Law (that's Medicare/Medicaid), but:
- Many states have dental board rules about soliciting referrals
- Some states have Dentist-Patient Anti-Solicitation rules
- The FTC has scrutinized referral platforms in healthcare
- If CaseLink ever adds any financial incentive for a specialist to receive referrals (even implicit tiers where "premium" specialists get surfaced first), you may have a problem

Specifically dangerous product features to avoid without legal review:
- Paid placement for specialists in GP's specialist directory
- Any algorithmic ranking that correlates with specialist spending on your platform
- Any feature that looks like a referral fee arrangement

Get a healthcare regulatory attorney review before you build anything that affects referral routing. Cost: $3,000-$5,000 for a memo. Worth it.

State dental board rules vary: Virginia, Maryland, and DC all have different rules about dentist advertising, patient solicitation, and referral arrangements. Your marketing language ("grow your referral network") should be reviewed by someone who knows each jurisdiction.

#2: The Fax Machine Problem

Roughly 70% of dental practices still use fax machines for some communications. This isn't going away for 5+ years. The temptation is to say "just switch to us" — that's wrong. Your real play is:
- Build a HIPAA-compliant fax-to-digital gateway so specialists can receive CaseLink referrals even if the GP sends a fax
- Build an email-to-referral parser so practices that email referrals can be auto-imported
- Build a Chrome extension or PMS integration so GPs can generate a CaseLink referral from within their existing PMS without switching tabs

The goal: reduce the behavior change required to zero. Don't ask them to change — make CaseLink invisible in their existing workflow.

#3: Data Ownership Edge Cases

Your HIPAA compliance is table stakes. But there are edge cases you probably haven't stress-tested:
- Patient disputes access to their own referral records — who owns the referral data, the GP, the specialist, or the patient?
- A specialist leaves the platform — what happens to historical referral data? GPs still want access to that history.
- A DSO acquires a CaseLink user — does the data migrate? Does the DSO get visibility into the independent specialist's referral relationships?
- A GP practice gets acquired — their CaseLink account and referral history is a business asset. Have a data portability story.

Get a BAA (Business Associate Agreement) template that clearly specifies data ownership and portability. This becomes important in every DSO conversation.

#4: Churn Vectors You Haven't Anticipated

The obvious churn is "they stop paying" — but the non-obvious ones are:
- Staff turnover — the front desk coordinator who set up CaseLink leaves, new hire doesn't know about it, referrals go back to phone/fax
- The GP who signed up leaves the practice — the specialist loses their referral source and doesn't see ongoing value
- Seasonal variability — specialist practices can have 20-30% revenue swings. A bad summer = "let's cut expenses"
- Platform frustration after a single bad experience — if a referral gets lost or a specialist doesn't respond quickly, the GP blames CaseLink even if it's a human issue

Fix: Build notification flows that surface value even when users are passive. Weekly email: "You received 7 new referrals this week. 3 patients are scheduled." Specialists who see that email aren't churning.

#5: The Insurance Coordination Time Bomb

GPs often don't send insurance information with referrals. Specialists have to chase it down separately. This is a massive friction point that happens after the referral is sent — and right now, your platform probably doesn't help with it. When specialists realize CaseLink solves case tracking but doesn't solve the insurance chase, the ROI calculation weakens. This is a build-it-soon opportunity.

#6: Patient Experience Is Completely Missing

Right now CaseLink is B2B — GP to specialist. The patient is a data record, not a participant. This is a gap. Patients:
- Don't know their referral was sent
- Don't know which specialist to call
- Often don't follow through (30-40% referral drop-off rate)
- Don't get appointment reminders before their specialist visit

A patient-facing SMS/email notification layer ("Your dentist sent a referral to Dr. Smith. Click here to schedule.") would:
1. Dramatically improve referral completion rates (huge value to both GP and specialist)
2. Reduce no-shows at specialist appointments
3. Create a patient touchpoint that makes CaseLink visible and sticky

This feature alone could justify a price increase.

5. Product Opportunities Beyond the Roadmap

Ranked by impact and buildability:

Tier 1: Build This Year

1. Patient notification layer — SMS/email to patients when a referral is sent. Simple, builds huge value. A 10% improvement in referral completion rates at a specialist making $500K/year = $50K. Cost to you: HIPAA-compliant SMS (~$0.01-0.03/message). Revenue opportunity: include it in the specialist plan at no extra charge but cite it in ROI conversations.

2. Insurance snapshot on referral — When a GP sends a referral, auto-request (or prompt for) the patient's insurance card details. Store them in the case. Saves the specialist's front desk 10-15 minutes per new patient. This is table stakes to becoming irreplaceable.

3. Case notes + treatment history — GPs should be able to attach existing treatment notes (PDFs, x-rays). Specialists need clinical context, not just demographics. If you can make x-ray attachment easy (even just drag-and-drop PDF), you eliminate the "I'll just email the files" workaround.

4. Direct messaging within platform — Secure chat between GP and specialist about specific cases. This already exists per your website, but needs to be excellent. Every time a GP picks up the phone instead of using CaseLink messaging, you're losing stickiness.

Tier 2: Build in 12-18 Months

5. Scheduling integration — Already on your roadmap ("coming soon"). This is critical. If a GP can propose appointment times from within CaseLink, you eliminate the phone call entirely. Partner with scheduling APIs (Zocdoc for dental, or direct PMS integrations) rather than building your own scheduler.

6. Specialist capacity dashboard — Show GPs which specialists have earliest available appointments. This is high-value information. "Dr. Rodriguez has an opening Tuesday" versus "Dr. Chen is booked 6 weeks out." GPs will route to whoever can see the patient soonest. This is a powerful network effect reinforcer.

7. Outcome tracking and specialist ratings — Did the patient complete treatment? What was the case acceptance rate? GPs want to send patients to specialists they trust. If CaseLink surfaces outcome data, it becomes the authoritative source for specialist quality — which is enormously defensible.

Tier 3: 2027+ (Big Swings)

8. Insurance pre-auth automation — Connect to payer APIs to auto-submit pre-authorization requests when a referral is created. This is technically complex and requires payer partnerships, but the pain is extreme and no one has solved it well for dental. RCM players like DentalXChange have the payer connections; a partnership could shortcut development.

9. Referral analytics for GPs — "Here are your top 5 referral destinations, average case value by specialty, and referral completion rates." GPs who see this data become power users. Charge $49-99/month for this.

10. Data marketplace — Anonymized aggregate referral patterns, specialist capacity benchmarks, acceptance rates by geography. Sell to DSOs, insurance companies, dental consultants. Long-term, high-margin. Don't build until you have 500+ specialists.

8. Fundraising & Investor Positioning

What metrics matter most

Investors in B2B healthcare SaaS (and specifically two-sided marketplaces) look at:

  1. Net Revenue Retention (NRR) — Are your existing customers spending more each year? Target 110%+ NRR to be fundable for Series A. This means expansion revenue (from annual upgrades, add-on features, or price increases) exceeds churn.

  2. CAC Payback Period — How many months to recover your customer acquisition cost? Sub-18 months is good; sub-12 is excellent. At $449/month with 80% gross margin: if your sales + marketing cost to acquire one specialist is $3,000, payback is 3,000 / (449 × 0.8) = 8.4 months. That's very fundable.

  3. Network density metrics — Investors in marketplace companies will ask about "liquidity" — what % of referrals sent on the platform get accepted? What's the time to first appointment? These are proxy metrics for whether the network is working.

  4. Referral volume growth — Total referrals processed per month (not just active subscribers). This is the product metric that validates real usage.

  5. Specialist retention (logo churn) — Under 1.5% monthly churn is required for a healthy SaaS story. Track cohorts from Day 1.

Comparable exits/valuations in dental tech

  • Dental Intelligence (analytics/production tracking): Raised ~$100M Series B in 2022. Valued at ~$500M+.
  • Weave Communications (dental/healthcare communications): IPO'd at ~$2B valuation in 2021.
  • NexHealth (patient scheduling/communication): Raised at $1B+ valuation (unicorn) in 2021.
  • Archy: Raised ~$12M Series A in 2023.
  • Henry Schein OneS (dental PMS suite): Henry Schein's entire dental software segment is worth billions.
  • Carestream Dental division: Sold to private equity for $925M in 2017.

The pattern: Dental SaaS companies that achieve category leadership in a defined workflow (not everything) and build data moats command premium multiples (15-25x ARR). Companies that try to be full PMS replacements have more competition.

Seed: "We're replacing fax and phone tag for dental referrals. $50K MRR, 100 specialists in DMV, growing 15% MoM."

Series A: "We're the referral network for dental specialists. 400 specialists, $180K MRR, NRR of 115%. We own the GP-specialist communication layer and we're adding scheduling, insurance pre-auth, and patient notifications. Data flywheel beginning."

9. Team & Hiring Priorities

The 6 most critical roles right now (not all of these are hires)

1. Head of Sales / Sales Rep #1 (hire now)
The company lives or dies on the next 50 specialists. Nick should not be the only one selling. The first sales hire should be someone who has sold to dental practices before — ideally someone from Dental Intelligence, NexHealth, Weave, or a dental supply company. They understand the buying process (dentists are slow to decide but loyal once in), the objections ("I already have Dentrix"), and the right trade show circuit.

2. Customer Success / Onboarding Specialist (hire after 40 specialists)
Churn is the enemy. A dedicated onboarding person who calls every new specialist within 24 hours, walks them through setup, and checks in at day 30 will save 2-3 points of monthly churn. At $449/month LTV, retaining 3 extra customers/month = $1.3M+ in additional LTV. This pays for itself instantly.

3. Full-Stack Engineer (if not already in place)
You need someone who can build integrations fast — PMS APIs, SMS, scheduling hooks. A single strong full-stack engineer who knows healthcare integrations (HL7, FHIR, or at least dental EDI standards) is more valuable than a team of generalists.

4. Regulatory/Healthcare Compliance Advisor (fractional OK)
Not a full-time hire. A healthcare attorney or compliance consultant on a 10-hour/month retainer. You need them before you launch any new feature that touches referral routing or patient data.

5. Content/Community Lead (hire after $50K MRR)
Dental is a relationship industry. The person who runs your webinars, writes your "best practices for specialist referral growth" blog posts, and shows up at dental conferences is doing sales at scale. This is not marketing fluff — in dental, content authority translates directly to trust and sales.

6. Nick should NOT hire a CFO yet — You need a bookkeeper and Stripe dashboard. A CFO is a Series A concern.

Dental insider vs. SaaS operator debate

Hire dental insiders for sales and customer success. Hire SaaS operators for product and growth. The mistake most healthcare SaaS founders make is hiring all insiders who can't ship fast, or all SaaS operators who don't understand why dentists are different from regular SMBs.

Dentists are:
- Risk-averse (their income is tied to their hands, literally)
- Relationship-driven (they trust peers more than vendors)
- Time-poor (15-minute lunch breaks aren't a stereotype)
- Status-conscious (the "leading specialist" framing works)

Your sales motion needs to work within these constraints. Cold email alone won't work. Peer referrals, dental society presentations, and CE sponsorships work.

What Nick should NOT hire for yet

  • VP of Marketing — premature. Nick should be doing direct sales.
  • Business Development Director — too early to manage partnerships at scale.
  • Data Science / ML — you don't have enough data yet.
  • Enterprise Sales — the DSO motion requires a specialized playbook you haven't built yet.
  • Any C-suite title that requires a $150K+ salary — burn it on engineering and sales capacity instead.

10. Three Things That Could Kill This Company

#1: The "Good Enough" Competitor Problem

The most likely failure mode is not a direct competitor building a better product — it's an existing PMS vendor adding a basic referral feature as a bundled add-on and making CaseLink feel redundant.

Example: Archy launches "Archy Referrals" — a basic version of what CaseLink does, included free in their existing $600/month plan. GPs and specialists who already use Archy stop evaluating CaseLink. Dentrix adds a "smart referral" module to Dentrix Ascend (their cloud product). Suddenly, "why do I need CaseLink?" becomes the sales objection you can't easily overcome.

Mitigation: Build features so sticky that switching costs are real. Outcome tracking, historical referral data, and the GP network are impossible to replicate overnight. If a specialist has 2 years of referral analytics in CaseLink, they're not migrating to a basic PMS module.

#2: Network Density Failure in the First 3 Markets

The network effect is the moat. But network effects can run in reverse — if specialists on the platform aren't receiving high-quality referrals (because not enough GPs are active), they won't renew. If they don't renew, GP adoption slows. You can spiral down as fast as you spiral up.

This is the most likely actual failure mode, not a dramatic competitor story. It's slow-motion: 20% of specialists don't renew because their referral volume from the platform is disappointing. Word gets out. New specialist acquisition slows.

Mitigation: Actively manage the supply side. When a new specialist joins, personally connect them with 5-10 GPs in their area who should be referring to them. Don't wait for the marketplace to work organically — it won't in the early days. White-glove the first 100.

#3: Running Out of Runway Before Product-Market Fit Is Clear

At $399-499/month with fewer than 100 specialists, CaseLink is generating under $500K ARR. That's not enough to be self-sustaining if there's more than a 2-person team. The burn rate vs. growth rate math is brutal at this stage.

If the 100-specialist milestone slips to 2027, and the first seed round isn't closed, there's a real danger of losing key engineers to higher-paying companies or having to take unfavorable terms from investors when desperate.

What Nick should lose sleep over: His cash runway vs. the time to reach $50K MRR. If current runway is under 12 months, he needs to be fundraising now, not after hitting the milestone. Raise while things are working, not when they have to.

11. The Big Idea

The Dental Referral Network Becomes a Clinical Intelligence Network

Here's the contrarian move: Stop thinking of CaseLink as a coordination tool. Start building it as the authoritative source of truth for specialist outcomes and referral intelligence in dentistry.

Here's what that means in practice:

Every referral processed through CaseLink creates a data point: who referred, which specialty, what procedure was anticipated, what actually happened, how long the patient waited, whether pre-auth was obtained, what the outcome was (case acceptance, treatment completion, specialist re-referral back to GP). Over 3-5 years, with 10,000+ specialists and 100,000+ GPs in the network, CaseLink will know:

  • Which specialists in every metro have the highest case acceptance rates
  • Where GP-to-specialist communication breaks down and why
  • What insurance pre-auth patterns predict approval vs. denial
  • How DSO affiliation affects referral completion rates
  • Which GP/specialist pairs have the most productive relationships

This data has value to:
- Insurers — "What's the expected specialist utilization per 1,000 GP patients?" Delta Dental would pay for this.
- DSOs — "Which specialists in our referral network are performing?" DSOs acquiring practices want this.
- Dental schools — "Where are the care coordination gaps in community dental care?"
- Investors in dental practices — Practice acquisition funds need data on specialist referral networks.
- Patients (eventually) — "Your dentist recommends 3 specialists. Here's their acceptance rate, average wait time, and patient satisfaction."

The 5-year vision if everything goes right:

CaseLink is not a referral coordination platform. It's the Dental Intelligence Network — the company that owns the workflow layer connecting every GP and specialist in America, and generates the most authoritative dataset on dental care pathways ever assembled. In this future:
- Annual revenue: $50-100M
- Customers: 10,000+ specialists + DSO enterprise contracts + data licensing to 3+ insurers
- Strategic value: Enormous. Any PMS vendor, insurer, or DSO trying to understand dental care patterns needs to either buy or partner with CaseLink.
- Acquirer: Henry Schein, Delta Dental, or a private equity roll-up at $500M-$1B valuation.

The moat in this scenario isn't the software — it's the data flywheel. Every referral processed makes the platform smarter about dental care patterns. That data compounds over time in a way that no competitor can replicate by building a better form.

The non-obvious strategic move to start this: Build outcome tracking now, even before it's fully automated. After a specialist completes a case, send a 2-question notification to the referring GP: "Did this patient complete treatment? Was the case what you expected?" That data is gold. It takes 6 weeks to build and it starts the flywheel.

The 5 Things I'd Do in the First 30 Days as an Employee

Day 1-5: Align on the price.
Pick $399 or $499. Make the website, sales deck, and contracts consistent. If it's $399/month, fine — but build in a 6-month price lock followed by an increase to $499 for new customers. Have that conversation with every current customer before they renew. Confused pricing is a trust killer in every deal.

Day 6-10: Call every specialist who's paying, personally.
Not a survey. Not an NPS email. A 20-minute phone call. "What would make you not renew? What's the #1 thing you wish CaseLink did that it doesn't?" Document it. The product roadmap for the next 6 months should come from these calls. Also: ask which GPs they most want to connect with — then call those GPs and get them to activate their accounts.

Day 11-15: Map the competitive threat precisely.
Get someone to test-drive Archy, NexHealth, and Dentrix Ascend and document their current referral features, their pricing, and where they're headed. The landscape changes fast. This isn't something to do once — it should be a quarterly exercise.

Day 16-20: Build the patient notification MVP.
A HIPAA-compliant SMS to the patient saying "Your dentist sent a referral to [Specialist Name]. Here's their contact info." This is 3-4 days of engineering work. It dramatically improves referral completion rates (which specialists will see in their analytics) and gives CaseLink a patient-facing touchpoint that no one else has. Demo this feature in every sales call going forward.

Day 21-30: Land the first dental school pilot.
Email the Dean of Clinical Affairs at Howard University College of Dentistry, University of Maryland, and VCU. Offer CaseLink free for their inter-clinic referral workflow. If one says yes, it's a case study, a referral pipeline of residents-turned-practitioners, and a press release that validates the platform in an institutional setting. This is a 30-day effort for a 5-year return.

This analysis was prepared with the full intent of being useful, not comfortable. The opportunity is real. The risks are manageable. The biggest risk of all is moving too slowly.
